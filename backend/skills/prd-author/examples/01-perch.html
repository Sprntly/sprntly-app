Automatic Invoice Follow-Up

AuthorDavid Mumuni
Context

Perch is invoicing software for freelancers and micro-agencies (1–10 people): designers, developers, consultants, and small studios who bill clients directly. A user creates an invoice in Perch, sends it by email, and records payment when it arrives — either manually or via a connected Stripe account. Perch's involvement ends at "send": there is no product behavior between an invoice going out and a payment being recorded. The gap between those two moments is where this document lives; the time it spans is called days-to-paid, and everything an invoice sender does during it — reminder emails, awkward nudges — currently happens outside the product.

Problem

Scope assumption: follow-up on already-sent invoices only — not payment processing, collections, or subscription dunning.

Late payments are the top pain in user interviews, but Perch does nothing after "send." For the business, users churn to tools that "handle the awkward part"; for the user, chasing money is uncomfortable, so they delay — and get paid later.

Users
  1. Freelancer — hates the chasing email more than the late payment; wants it handled without sounding aggressive to a client she needs next quarter.
  2. Micro-agency owner — 30+ open invoices; needs to see which are being chased without managing each one.
Goal
Primary metricMedian days-to-paid = median(paid_date − sent_date) · Baseline: 24 days
Projected impactblank — fills after pilot; no reminder-effectiveness data for this user base
GuardrailsClient unsubscribe/complaint rate on reminders · invoice send volume (no chilling effect)
Hypothesis

If Perch sends polite, scheduled reminders automatically after an invoice passes due — with the user approving tone once, not per email — late invoices will get paid sooner without damaging client relationships.

Non-goals

Payment processing changes · collections/escalation to agencies · SMS reminders (V2 candidate).

Risks

Reminders that feel robotic damage the client relationship the freelancer is protecting; partial payments create ambiguous states.

Riskiest assumption — exactly one

Clients pay faster because of reminders — rather than paying on their own AP schedule regardless.

Pre-mortem: It's six weeks later and this failed because reminded and unreminded invoices paid on the same curve. The earliest signal was no separation in the pilot cohort by week three. We should have run a holdout group from day one.

Alignment

Attacks the top interview pain and the named churn driver.

Rollout

Opt-in beta to 100 accounts with a holdout cohort.

Done-when

Reminder pipeline live, holdout comparison readable, unsubscribe rate < 1%.
