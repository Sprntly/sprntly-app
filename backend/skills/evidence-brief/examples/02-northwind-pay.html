Get SMBs Paid Faster, and They'll Stay

Invoices without a pay-now link get paid 12 days slower, and slow payment is the strongest predictor of churn.

Author: [Author name], Data Science  ·  2026-06-23  ·  Status: Draft  ·  Pairs with → "Embedded Pay-Now" PRD

Example brief — Northwind Pay is a hypothetical company; all figures illustrative.

TL;DR

Three of every ten invoices our SMBs send aren't paid within 30 days. The single biggest lever is the pay-now link: invoices that include one are paid in 16 days vs 28 without. And the accounts whose invoices go unpaid longest are the ones that churn. The data, support, and sales are all pointing at the same fix — make getting paid effortless inside the invoice.

OPPORTUNITY

Embed a pay-now link in every invoice by default — the change most likely to speed up payment and, with it, reduce churn.

Context. Northwind Pay is invoicing software for small businesses; the goal this informs is net revenue retention. SMBs send invoices and wait to get paid — and how long they wait turns out to track closely with whether they stick around.

WHERE IT LEAKS

A third of invoices stall before they're paid.

Following invoices through the payment funnel, most get opened — but a large share never reach "paid" inside 30 days. That stall is where the money, and the customer's patience, leaks out.

THE LEVER — AND THE WEDGE

One field changes the wait by 12 days.

Comparing invoices sent with and without an embedded pay-now link (the latter from a limited beta), the difference is large and consistent: a median of 28 days to payment without it, 16 with. Faster payment is exactly what the at-risk accounts need.

"My clients say they don't know how to pay me through the invoice — can I add a card button?"

Sales call · churned

WHY IT MATTERS

Slow payment leads churn up.

Monthly logo churn has climbed as the share of slow-paid accounts grew. The accounts that wait longest to get paid are over-represented among those that leave — payment friction isn't just an annoyance, it's a retention problem.

THE CONVERGENCE

Three signals, one fix.

The data, our support queue, and our sales team arrived at the same answer separately. I don't have a strong competitive or market read here yet — so this rests on three signals, not five, and I'm holding confidence accordingly. It's enough to test.
