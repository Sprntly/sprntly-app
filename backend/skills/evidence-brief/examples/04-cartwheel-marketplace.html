More Photos, More Sales: The Listing-Quality Opportunity

Listings with three or more photos convert 2.4× — and almost half our sellers add only one.

Author: [Author name], Data Science  ·  2026-06-23  ·  Status: Draft  ·  Pairs with → "Guided Listing" PRD

Example brief — Cartwheel is a hypothetical company; all figures illustrative.

TL;DR

Conversion climbs sharply with the number of listing photos, leveling off around three. Yet nearly half of all listings have just one photo — so a large share of our inventory is under-converting for a fixable reason. This is a single, strong data signal; I don't have customer or competitive corroboration, so I'm presenting it as a one-signal finding worth validating, not a sure thing.

OPPORTUNITY

Get more sellers to three good photos at listing time — the cheapest visible lever on conversion in the dataset.

Context. Cartwheel is a consumer resale marketplace; the goal this informs is gross merchandise value (GMV). Sellers create listings with photos and a description. I looked at what, in a listing, most moves the odds of a sale.

THE SIGNAL

Conversion rises with photos, then plateaus at three.

Bucketing listings by photo count, conversion more than triples from one photo to three, then flattens. Three is the point of diminishing returns — a clear, actionable target.

THE GAP

Most sellers stop at one.

The distribution shows where the opportunity sits: nearly half of listings have a single photo, far below the point where conversion takes off. The gap between where sellers are and where conversion peaks is the addressable upside.

CONVERGENCE — NOT YET

One signal, named as one signal.

This rests entirely on product data. I don't have voice-of-customer (we don't survey sellers at listing time) or a competitive read on listing flows, so there's nothing to triangulate against yet. The effect is strong and consistent, but a single correlational signal can hide a confound — for instance, more-engaged sellers may both add photos and price better. I'd want a controlled test before treating it as causal.
