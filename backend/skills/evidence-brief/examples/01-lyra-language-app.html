Five independent signals converge on one bet at the beginner→intermediate plateau.

Example brief — Lyra is a hypothetical company; all figures here are illustrative.

TL;DR

Week-4 retention has fallen to ~17%, and the loss isn't spread out — it's concentrated at the jump from beginner drills into intermediate content. When I cross-checked that against what customers say, what sales hears, what competitors ship, and where the market is moving, all of it pointed at one missing capability: a way to actually speak. The cohort that did reach a beta speaking moment retained at 2.3×. I think there's a clear bet here.

OPPORTUNITY

Own the moment beginners plateau by letting them start speaking — the one capability the data, our users, our lost deals, and our competitors all independently point to.

Context. Lyra teaches languages through short tap-and-match drills; the goal this informs is subscription retention and LTV. New learners are highly active in week one, then most are gone by week four. This brief looks across data, customers, sales, competitors, and the market to find where they leave, why, and the bet worth testing.

WHERE THEY LEAVE — AND WHO DIDN'T

Retention falls off a cliff at the intermediate gate. One cohort didn't.

I started with the new-user retention curve (left). It declines steadily, then steepens sharply between week two and week four — and when I broke that down by stage, nearly a third of all churn lands at a single point: the transition out of beginner units. Then one cut stopped me (right): beta users who reached a speaking moment at that transition retained at more than double everyone else. I want to be clear that this is correlational — which is exactly why I'm bringing it as a hypothesis to test, not a result to act on blindly.

WHY — IN CUSTOMERS' OWN WORDS

Three different channels, one recurring ask.

I theme-coded the negative feedback to see if the data had an explanation. "Repetitive" is the loudest complaint, but the one that aligns with the retention cliff is the second: people don't get to speak. The same need shows up whether someone leaves a review, files a ticket, or tells our sales team why they left.

WHAT THE MARKET IS TELLING US

Rivals are investing exactly where we leak.

I checked whether this is just us. It isn't — and the competitive picture is itself a signal. Every serious competitor now ships speaking practice; we're the only one without it, and the only one with no adaptive support at the plateau where our users quit. Separately, AI voice has made real-time speaking practice cheap to build and something learners now expect by default.

THE CONVERGENCE — why I believe it

Five signals, gathered separately, agree.

This is what moved me from "interesting" to "worth a bet." Each of these came from a different place and a different method, and none was looking for the others. When they line up this cleanly on one capability, the agreement is the argument — more than any single chart above.

MY RECOMMENDATION — THE BET TO TEST

Value-driven hypothesis

We believe that by introducing adaptive conversation practice at the beginner→intermediate transition, learners will start speaking early and feel real progress — and that behavior change will drive higher week-4 retention and subscription renewals (LTV).

Behavior change I'd expect: more learners reach a first speaking moment in week 2–3.  Business value: retention → renewals → LTV; secondary lift in DAU.  How I'd test it: randomized experiment on the transition cohort; primary metric week-4 retention vs control; guardrails: lesson completion, CSAT; pre-register the minimum lift with the team before launch.
