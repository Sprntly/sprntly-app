TL;DR

Synthesized from 517 pieces of first-party feedback across 38 accounts between 20 January and 20 July 2026 — our own tickets, named-account Slack threads, nine renewal and win-loss calls, and in-product feedback. Ordered by how frustrated customers sounded, as asked.

Engineers are not asking for more features. What they describe are five problems that make them stop trusting what Cassette tells them, listed hottest first:

#1Engineers cannot tell whether a missing trace is a bug in their code or a trace Cassette threw away. Sampling drops spans with no visible record, so during an incident the tool becomes a thing to double-check rather than a thing to trust. "We spent ninety minutes of a Sev1 chasing a span that never existed. Your sampler ate it and told nobody. I cannot run an incident on a tool that lies by omission." — Fernwood Systems
#2Teams cannot upgrade the SDK without budgeting for breakage. Minor version bumps break instrumentation, so upgrades get deferred, and deferred upgrades mean they are permanently behind on the fixes they are asking for. "A patch bump took out our Go instrumentation in staging. We now pin everything and upgrade twice a year, in a room, together." — Kestrel Labs
#3Platform teams cannot manage alerting the way they manage everything else. Alert rules exist only in the UI, so there is no review, no diff, and no rollback — and the platform team that would standardize Cassette across the company won't adopt a tool that sits outside their pipeline. "Everything we own is in Terraform. Alerts are the one thing a human clicks into a form, and that is the reason we haven't rolled you out past two teams." — Bracken Financial
#4Anyone investigating a slow-burn problem is locked out of the data they need. Queries over more than about seven days time out, which is exactly the range you need for a regression that crept in over a fortnight. "Thirty-day query, spinner, gone. So I export to BigQuery and do it there. Which rather raises the question of what I'm paying you for." — Halden Retail Group
#5Owners cannot answer finance when asked which team is spending the ingest budget. Cost arrives as one company-wide number, so the platform owner absorbs a quarterly argument they have no data to settle. "Finance asks me every quarter which team is burning the budget. I tell them I don't know. That is not a great look for me." — Sundara Logistics

What this means for us: the top two are trust problems, not capability gaps — both churns this half cited one of them, taking $105K. The reach picture is different from the heat picture: query timeouts affect the most accounts (22 of 38) but rank fourth on frustration, while dropped traces affect 14 and rank first. And the single largest commercial item on the board is the one customers are calmest about — alert-rules-as-code gates $220K of platform-team expansion.

Recommendations

The themes above are ordered by frustration, as you asked. These recommendations are ordered by fit to the goal metrics this team tracks — weekly active querying teams (North Star), gross retention, ingest expansion, time-to-first-alert. The two orderings disagree, and that is useful: alert-rules-as-code is fourth on heat and second here.
1
Surface sampling loss — show what was dropped, and let teams control it
Why: the hottest theme by a distance and a trust problem rather than a feature gap. Customers are explicit that visibility alone would largely settle it, which suggests a cheaper fix than it first appears.
Moves: weekly active querying teams ↑ · gross retention ↑
2
Alert rules as code — Terraform provider and YAML import/export
Why: only fourth on frustration, but $220K of named expansion is gated on it and it is the specific blocker keeping us a two-team tool inside large orgs. Goal fit elevates it above its heat.
Moves: ingest expansion ↑ · weekly active teams ↑ (new teams onboarded)
3
Make long-range queries complete — or fail honestly and partially
Why: widest reach in the report, one churn, and nine accounts have built export pipelines that permanently move their most valuable questions off Cassette. Partial results with an honest marker would recover most of the value ahead of a full performance rebuild.
Moves: weekly active querying teams ↑ · gross retention ↑
4
Stabilize the SDK contract — semver discipline and an instrumentation compatibility test suite
Why: joint-hottest theme, and it compounds: pinned teams can't receive the fixes above. Smallest reach of the four, and the cheapest to prevent going forward.
Moves: time-to-first-alert ↓ · gross retention ↑
Deliberately not recommended: per-team cost attribution (17 accounts, 45% — the second-widest theme in the report) and the three minor requests. Cost attribution is wide but calm, has no churn signal, no stated renewal conditionality, and is fundamentally a billing-transparency question rather than a product capability — it goes to pricing-packaging. Promoting it here on reach alone would push a genuine trust problem down the list to answer someone else's finance meeting.

Any of these four is ready to be developed into a PRD.
