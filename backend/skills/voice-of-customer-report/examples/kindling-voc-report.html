The program launches, then quietly stops.

Asked: "Give me the voice of customer report for the last two quarters."
Scope: all sources · all accounts · full window (no filters applied) · Sources: 14 recorded CSM/QBR calls · 3 churn exit interviews · 6 sales expansion & win-loss calls · 218 support tickets · 143 in-product feedback submissions · 96 NPS verbatims · Coverage: 41 accounts of a 128-account base (32%) · 683 records captured, 570 counted as customer evidence · Goal & metrics: make recognition a durable habit, not a launch event — tracked on weekly recognition participation rate (North Star), gross retention, net-revenue-retention, and time-to-first-value

TL;DR

Synthesized from 570 pieces of first-party feedback across 41 accounts between 6 January and 30 June 2026 — fourteen CSM and QBR calls, three churn exit interviews, six expansion and win-loss conversations, plus our own tickets, in-product feedback and NPS verbatims.

Almost nobody says the product is bad. What they describe are four problems they cannot solve on their own:

#1Sponsors have no way to make a manager who has never posted, post. Within eight weeks of launch recognition collapses onto six or eight enthusiasts, and the only tool the sponsor has left is chasing people personally — which they can't sustain, so the program goes quiet. "By March it was the same four managers recognising the same twelve people. I was personally DMing directors to please post something." — Northwind Health (churned)
#2Sponsors can't defend the spend when finance challenges it. They are asked what the money bought and have nothing exportable to answer with — no retention link, no survey movement, nothing a CFO reads as a number. "My CFO asked what forty-one thousand dollars bought us. I had a screenshot of a leaderboard. That was the whole meeting." — Alto Legal (churned)
#3Employees outside the US receive a visibly worse program than their US colleagues. The rewards catalogue thins out badly abroad, so the sponsor is left choosing between excluding a whole region and offering it something that lands as an insult. "My Manila team sees four options and my Chicago team sees four hundred. I've had people ask me to stop including them." — Vantage Bio
#4Sponsors are paying for a program they are not allowed to launch. Payroll won't approve go-live without taxable-reward reporting they can process, and the sponsor has no way to produce it — so the contract runs while the product sits unused. "We signed in February. We have not gone live. Payroll won't sign off and I can't give them a file that works." — Quarrystone Group

What this means for us: all three churns this half were participation-decay stories, not product-quality ones — $185K gone from accounts that renewed happily the year before. Another $286K in renewals sits with sponsors who currently have no way to defend the spend, $340K of named expansion is gated on the international catalogue, and $88K is paid-but-unlaunched behind payroll. The throughline is that we sell a habit and ship a launch: we're strong at week one and absent by month four.

Recommendations

Selected by fit to the goal metrics this team tracks — weekly recognition participation rate (North Star), gross retention, net-revenue-retention, and time-to-first-value — not by how many accounts raised something or how angrily. Four make the cut; one wide theme deliberately does not.
1
Build manager activation — nudges, prompts, and a participation view the sponsor can act on
Why: the only theme sitting behind two of our three churns, and the one that directly attacks the North Star. Every other fix matters less if the habit still dies in month four.
Moves: weekly participation ↑ · gross retention ↑
2
Ship an exportable program-impact brief for the HR sponsor
Why: $286K renews within two quarters with sponsors who currently cannot defend the spend, and this is the angriest theme in the report. Narrow scope, renewal-cycle timing, and it turns a believer into an advocate.
Moves: gross retention ↑ · value visibility ↑
3
Expand the rewards catalogue by region — start EMEA and APAC
Why: the calmest serious theme and the largest commercial upside. $340K of named expansion is waiting on it, and it is the only fix that moves participation for the employees currently getting the worse product.
Moves: net-revenue-retention ↑ · participation (non-US) ↑
4
Produce a payroll-ready taxable-rewards report
Why: $88K is paid and has never gone live, and these nine accounts are as frustrated as any in the report. Smallest theme in the top four and probably the smallest build — it unblocks revenue we have already booked.
Moves: time-to-first-value ↓ · launch completion ↑
Deliberately not recommended: the Slack/Teams surface (13 accounts, second-widest reach) and the four minor requests. The chat theme is wide but calm — no churn, no dollars, frustration 2 of 5, and only an asserted link to participation. Promoting it on reach alone is exactly the failure this ranking is meant to avoid. It goes to experiment-design to establish whether the participation link is real; if it is, it returns as a first-tier recommendation with evidence behind it.

Any of these four is ready to be developed into a PRD.
