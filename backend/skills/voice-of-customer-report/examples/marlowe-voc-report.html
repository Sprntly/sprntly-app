TL;DR

Synthesized from 264 support tickets raised by 19 enterprise accounts between 1 March and 18 July 2026. This is a single-source, single-segment slice by request — tickets skew toward things that break rather than things that are merely missing, and calls and survey verbatims were excluded.

Every problem enterprise legal teams raised sits in the last mile between a drafted contract and an executed one:

#1Lawyers cannot find a clause they know exists, so they rewrite it from scratch. Clause search matches on exact wording only, so the approved fallback language is invisible unless you remember what it was called — and a re-drafted clause has to go back through review. "I searched limitation of liability. Nothing. It's filed as liability cap. I wrote a new one, and now legal has to approve a clause we already approved in 2024." — Ashgrove Partners
#2Approvals stop dead when an approver is on leave. Routing ignores the delegate configured for out-of-office, so contracts sit unmoved until somebody notices manually. "Two weeks. It sat in a queue for two weeks because our GC was on annual leave and the delegate we configured was ignored." — Trentham Industries
#3Redlines come out of Marlowe unusable in Word, so counsel does the round trip by hand. Tracked changes and comment threads are mangled on bulk export, which breaks the one workflow outside counsel will not give up. "Exported eleven redlines for outside counsel. Every one had the tracked changes flattened and the comments detached. I rebuilt them by hand over a weekend." — Calderwood Group
#4Compliance teams cannot get the evidence they are required to produce. Audit history is visible on screen and cannot be exported, so it gets screenshotted into a spreadsheet before every review. "Our auditors want an immutable record. I have screenshots in a spreadsheet. I have to explain that to them, annually." — Ashgrove Partners

What this means for us: none of these is a gap in what Marlowe can do — all four are places where the work leaves the product and comes back by hand. Every one of them adds days to execution time, which is the North Star. Two accounts in this slice have open escalations tied to items #2 and #3.

Recommendations

Selected by fit to the goal metrics this team tracks — contracts executed per active customer per month (North Star), gross retention, enterprise seat expansion — not by how many accounts raised something. All four themes sit on the execution path, so all four are recommended; the ordering differs from the prevalence ordering above.
1
Fix bulk redline export — preserve tracked changes and comment threads
Why: the hottest theme by a distance, four accounts raising it five or more times each, and it breaks the one workflow outside counsel will not abandon. Third by prevalence, first by goal fit — it removes a hand-rebuild step from every external review cycle.
Moves: contracts executed per month ↑ · gross retention ↑
2
Honor out-of-office delegation in approval routing
Why: two open escalations and the most direct line to execution time in the report — a two-week stall is two weeks of North Star. The delegate setting already exists and is simply ignored by routing, so this is likely the smallest build here.
Moves: contracts executed per month ↑
3
Make clause search find synonyms and aliases — not a new clause feature
Why: most prevalent problem, and it doubles review work by pushing already-approved language back through approval. Scope it as search and aliasing over the existing library; scoping it as new clause functionality would be expensive and would miss the actual problem.
Moves: contracts executed per month ↑ · gross retention ↑
4
Ship an exportable, signed audit trail
Why: least prevalent of the four but a compliance obligation rather than a preference, and it is a recurring reason enterprise buyers involve their security and audit functions. Narrow, well-specified, and unblocks a procurement conversation rather than a daily one.
Moves: enterprise expansion ↑ · gross retention ↑
Deliberately not recommended: template version history and SSO group mapping lag. Both are real and both are cheap, but neither touches execution time, retention, or expansion, and neither was raised with any heat. They stay on monitor rather than taking a slot from the four above.
One caveat on this ranking. This run had volume and frustration only — support tickets carry no account status or ARR, so nothing here is weighted by what an account is worth or how close it is to leaving. Two of these four could reorder once churn and commercial data are joined, and the empty churn and $ columns above should not be read as an absence of risk. Re-running unfiltered, or joining CRM data, would settle it.

Any of these four is ready to be developed into a PRD.
