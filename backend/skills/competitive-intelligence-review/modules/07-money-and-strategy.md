# Module 7 — Money & strategy (public-company filings)

**Question:** For public (or well-funded) competitors — how's the business actually doing, and what does their spending reveal about their strategy?

**Why.** Filings and earnings calls are the highest-confidence source you have (audited, dated). They reveal the real strategy — where revenue is growing, what they're acquiring, where R&D is going, and how they're broadening into your space — better than any marketing page.

**Method (per public competitor)**
1. **Revenue & growth:** total and segment revenue, YoY growth, and *which segment* is growing (often the one encroaching on you). Source: 10-K/10-Q (US, free on SEC EDGAR), annual reports, earnings releases.
2. **Profitability & efficiency:** gross margin, operating margin, trend — tells you how much room they have to cut price or out-invest.
3. **R&D & where the money goes:** R&D spend and *stated priorities* — read the MD&A and earnings-call transcripts (free: SEC, Motley Fool/Seeking Alpha transcripts) for what leadership says they're betting on.
4. **Acquisitions & broadening:** M&A in the last 1–2 years — what capability or segment each acquisition buys them. This is the clearest signal of where they're expanding (e.g. an incumbent acquiring an AI startup to enter your lane).
5. **Stated strategy:** pull the actual language from the annual letter / call about their direction; quote sparingly, paraphrase the thesis.

**For private competitors:** use funding rounds (Crunchbase free), investor announcements, headcount growth (LinkedIn), and job postings as proxies; label everything as estimate/inference.

**Output:** per public competitor — growth/margin trend, R&D direction, M&A moves, and the one-paragraph "what their money says their strategy is."
**Hand to module 8:** the best-resourced threat and the direction they're broadening — the move you must get ahead of.
**Confidence:** filings 🅗 hard (cite the filing + date); private-company proxies 🅢/🅘.
