# Module 4 — Product & pricing teardown

**Question:** Feature-for-feature and price-for-price, where does each player win and lose — by the *job the customer is hiring the product for*, not a checklist?

**Why JTBD, not a feature checklist.** A feature-parity table is the tar pit — it makes you chase features instead of value. Compare on *jobs*: who delivers each job best, and where the gaps are.

**Method**
1. **List the core jobs** the buyer hires this category for (e.g. "decide what to build", "track delivery", "communicate status"). 4–8 jobs.
2. **Score each player per job:** wins / competitive / loses — with the *why* (the capability behind it), not just a checkmark.
3. **Pricing & packaging analysis:** entry price, tiers, what gates each tier, the pricing *metric* (per seat / usage / flat), and free/trial motion. Note where each is positioned (premium/value/freemium) and what that signals.
4. **Map price to value:** who's over/under-priced for the jobs they win; where your pricing needs a justification narrative (e.g. premium price defended by a job only you do).

**Data:** product = each site's product pages, docs, changelogs, demo videos, G2/Capterra feature grids. Pricing = public pricing pages (and archived versions via the Wayback Machine to see pricing *trend*).
**Output:** a jobs × players matrix (win/competitive/lose + why) and a pricing/packaging comparison with the value-map read.
**Hand to module 8:** the jobs where we win (lean in / position on) and lose (close or cede); pricing moves.
**Confidence:** features 🅗 (public); "who wins a job" 🅘 (judgment) — label.
