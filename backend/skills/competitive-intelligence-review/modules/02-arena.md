# Module 2 — The arena (Porter's Five Forces)

**Question:** Who and what do we really compete with — including substitutes and future entrants?

**Method — Five Forces, read for product implications:**
1. **Competitive rivalry:** how many, how concentrated, how intense. High + fragmented → differentiation must be structural, not feature.
2. **Threat of substitutes:** the *other ways* to do the job — incl. DIY (a GPT prompt + a spreadsheet), "do nothing", or an adjacent tool repurposed. Often the most underrated and most dangerous force.
3. **Threat of new entrants:** how easily others enter (esp. incumbents adding AI to an existing distribution base — the classic "every PM tool adds an AI feature" threat). Note barriers: data, network effects, switching costs.
4. **Buyer power:** how much leverage customers have (many alternatives, low switching cost → pricing pressure).
5. **Supplier power:** dependence on a platform, model provider, or data source that could squeeze you.

**Output:** each force rated low/med/high with a one-line product implication. The arena map: direct rivals · substitutes · adjacent/future entrants.
**Hand to module 8:** which forces most constrain strategy (e.g. "substitutes high → wedge must be a capability DIY can't replicate").
